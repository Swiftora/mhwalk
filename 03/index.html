Mullica Hill Historic Walking Tour

Stop Three 3

The Gaunt House

c. 1885 · 14 South Main Street

3-minute read · Stop 3 of 5

The man whose name is on this house was Mullica Hill's most consequential citizen, and his sphere was agriculture. George F. Gaunt led the New Jersey State Grange for sixteen years and in those years carried its membership from three thousand farmers to twenty-five thousand, becoming the most powerful agricultural voice in the state. The National Grange brought him onto its national stage as Lecturer and conferred on him its highest honorary degree, High Priest of Demeter. His standing in the trade earned him a seat on the New Jersey State Board of Agriculture, in a county that was running on tomatoes, sweet potatoes, asparagus, and apples, and a directorship at the Federal Reserve Bank of Philadelphia, where he was re-elected to a three-year term in 1915. His political career ran in parallel to the agricultural one. He served three terms in the New Jersey Senate, was elected its President, and acted as Governor of New Jersey in 1916. Through all of it he stayed inside this village. He was a volunteer firefighter at home. In September of 1918, fifty-two years old, the bell rang and he went. The injuries he took fighting that fire killed him at home.

The Senator's death did not end the working life of this house. His son and his daughter-in-law Marion inherited the property and lived in it for the next generation. When the Depression closed in, Marion went up to the attic with a sewing machine and started cutting fabric on the third floor while the country came apart below. Look up at the top story of the house now. The original two arched windows are gone. Marion had them rebuilt as three larger openings, because she needed the light to sew. Her work survived the Thirties. By the early 1940s she had a wartime contract and had outgrown the attic, and the operation expanded down the block into the Gaunt Store buildings. People in the village came to know her simply as Mrs. Gaunt.

This house carried generations of the same family. Marion outlived her father-in-law by seventy-six years and died in 1994 at age ninety-six. She is buried beside him in the Friends Burial Ground you just walked past. Before her death she carried the Senator's ceremonial Senate gavel down the street to the Old Town Hall museum where this tour began, and it remains in a case there today. The Gaunts kept this house for more than one hundred and twenty years. It changed hands outside the family for the first time in 2022. Look at the Church Street side of the building as you continue walking. The carriage step the Senator used to climb into a coach is still set in the grass between the sidewalk and the curb.

Did You Know Behind this house, the Senator's son built and operated a working live-steam railway. Visitors came from across the region to ride. The trains ran from this yard for decades.
